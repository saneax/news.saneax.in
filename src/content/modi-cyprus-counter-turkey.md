## The Chess Board Just Got Bigger

There's a quiet war being fought — not with bullets, but with bilateral agreements, defence roadmaps, and strategic partnerships. And India just moved a piece that Ankara didn't see coming.

When Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan chose to arm Pakistan during Operation Sindoor — sending drones, loitering munitions, and military hardware to be used against Indian forces — he bet that New Delhi would respond with diplomatic protests and nothing more. He was wrong.

India's answer isn't a press conference. It's Cyprus.

![India Cyprus Strategic Partnership](/content/images/modi-cyprus-header.jpg)

## What Just Happened

Cyprus President Nicos Christodoulides made a state visit to India at Prime Minister Modi's invitation, and the two nations didn't just exchange pleasantries. They **elevated their bilateral relationship to a strategic partnership** — diplomatic code for "we're serious about this."

The centrepiece: a **five-year defence cooperation roadmap (2026–2031)** covering security, maritime affairs, and cybersecurity. This is the third defence agreement between India and Cyprus since 2022, building on the initial pact signed during EAM S. Jaishankar's visit to Cyprus and fine-tuned during PM Modi's visit to Nicosia — notably, his first overseas visit after India's military victory over Pakistan.

Six agreements. Two MOUs. Joint working groups on counter-terrorism. Intelligence sharing. Search and rescue coordination. Maritime naval exchanges and port visits. A diplomatic training exchange between India's Sushma Swaraj Institute of Foreign Service and Cyprus's Diplomatic Academy.

This isn't diplomacy. This is architecture.

## Why Cyprus, and Why Now

Cyprus is Turkey's nemesis in the Mediterranean. The history is brutal and unambiguous: in 1974, Turkish forces invaded Cyprus, occupying the northern third of the island. To this day, Turkish troops remain deployed in what the international community — including the EU — considers illegally occupied territory. The self-declared "Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus" is recognized by exactly one country: Turkey.

This is the primary reason Turkey's EU accession talks have been frozen for decades. You don't get to occupy an EU member state's territory and then ask to join the club.

For India, the calculus is elegant:

- **Cyprus needs a powerful friend.** It faces an expansionist Turkey that disputes gas exploration rights, maintains military build-ups, and occupies its sovereign territory.
- **India needs a Mediterranean foothold.** Cyprus sits at the crossroads of Europe, the Middle East, and North Africa — a strategic listening post and logistical anchor.
- **Both share a common adversary's adversary dynamic.** Turkey arms Pakistan. India arms Cyprus. Geopolitics isn't complicated; it's chess.

## The Defence Angle: What India Brings to the Table

Here's where it gets interesting for defence watchers. Cyprus has reportedly expressed strong interest in acquiring **Indian military technology and hardware**, specifically:

- **Akash air defence systems** — the same systems that formed the backbone of India's air defence grid during Operation Sindoor
- **BrahMos supersonic cruise missiles** — the weapon that allowed the Indian Air Force to dominate the skies over Pakistan in under four days
- **Indian drone systems** — battle-tested, combat-proven

The subtext is unmistakable: Indian weapons that defeated Turkish-supplied drones in South Asia could now end up in the hands of the nation Turkey threatens in the Mediterranean.

An official speaking on the bilateral summit contextualised this within India's broader **security and defence partnership agreement with the European Union signed in January 2026**. Under the EU's €150 billion SAFE programme, Cyprus has been allocated €1.5 billion for defence modernization. Indian defence companies, now empowered by the EU partnership framework, can bid for these contracts alongside European firms.

This is the flywheel effect: strategic diplomacy → institutional frameworks → commercial defence deals → deeper military integration → stronger strategic alignment.

## The Turkey-Pakistan Military Axis: What India Is Countering

To understand India's Cyprus play, you need to understand what it's countering.

Turkey and Pakistan have deepened their military relationship dramatically in recent years. The numbers tell the story:

- **~10% of Turkey's total arms exports (2020–2024)** went to Pakistan, primarily drone-related equipment
- **Bayraktar TB2 MALE drones** — Turkey's signature export — were supplied to Pakistan
- **Anka-series surveillance drones** for ISR operations
- **MAM-L and MAM-C precision-guided munitions** from Roketsan
- **Kemankeş AI-powered cruise missile technology** from Baykar

During Operation Sindoor, Pakistan deployed hundreds of Turkish-origin drones and kamikaze UAVs against Indian targets. Indian air defences shot down **300–400 drones in a single night**, most of Turkish origin. Indian military leadership publicly displayed the debris of Turkish drones recovered from Indian territory.

Reports indicated that up to six Turkish C-130E Hercules transport aircraft carrying combat gear landed in Karachi during the conflict, tracked by open-source flight monitoring over the Arabian Sea. Ankara officially denied large-scale weapons shipments, calling reports "speculative" — but the wreckage on Indian soil told its own story.

Turkey has also consistently peddled Pakistan's narrative on Kashmir at international forums, despite New Delhi's repeated warnings to back off.

The pattern is clear: Erdoğan's Turkey is not a neutral party. It has chosen sides. India is now choosing sides too.

## The IMEC Connection: Economics as Strategy

The Cyprus partnership isn't purely military. Cyprus formally joined India's **Indo-Pacific Oceans Initiative** under the trade connectivity and maritime transport pillar. Both leaders emphasised the **India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC)** as a central pillar of the bilateral relationship.

Cyprus is one of the **top 10 investors in India**, and the two nations aim to **double investment in the next five years** — targeting pharmaceuticals, IT services, green energy, shipping, and maritime infrastructure. A new Cyprus trade office in Mumbai will anchor commercial ties.

PM Modi's framing was precise: "The strategic partnership between India and Cyprus will unlock capital for commerce by connecting the financial and services hubs of both countries."

Translation: We're not just selling weapons. We're building an economic corridor that connects India to Europe through a partner that happens to be Turkey's biggest headache.

## The Joint Statement's Quiet Bomb

Among the MOUs, one clause deserves attention: **Cyprus expressed "solidarity and unwavering support to India in its fight against cross-border terror."**

That's not generic diplomatic language. That's Cyprus — a Mediterranean island nation with no direct stake in South Asian geopolitics — explicitly aligning with India's position on Pakistan-sponsored terrorism. And it's India, in return, reaffirming its commitment to "the sovereignty and territorial integrity of all nations" — a direct reference to Turkey's occupation of Northern Cyprus.

Each supports the other on the issue that defines their respective security concerns. That's not coincidence. That's design.

## What This Means for India's Defence Posture

India's Cyprus strategy reveals a broader shift in New Delhi's approach to adversarial alliances:

**1. From reactive to proactive.** India is no longer just defending against Pakistan-Turkey military cooperation. It's creating countervailing pressure points. You arm our adversary? We'll empower yours.

**2. Defence exports as strategic tools.** The days of India being purely a defence importer are ending. Akash, BrahMos, and Indian drone systems are now instruments of foreign policy — offered to partners who align with Indian interests.

**3. European integration through bilateral backdoors.** The EU partnership agreement gives India institutional access to European defence markets. Cyprus becomes the proof-of-concept: an EU member state buying Indian military hardware through EU-approved channels.

**4. Maritime power projection.** Naval exchanges, port visits, and search-and-rescue coordination with Cyprus give India operational reach into the Eastern Mediterranean — a region where it previously had no military footprint.

**5. The IMEC multiplier.** Defence cooperation and economic connectivity reinforce each other. A militarised India-Cyprus relationship makes the IMEC corridor more secure, and a thriving economic corridor makes deeper defence integration politically sustainable.

## My Take

India's Cyprus move is the geopolitical equivalent of a judo throw — using your opponent's momentum against them.

Turkey spent years building a military partnership with Pakistan, flooding Islamabad with drones and missiles, championing the Kashmir cause at the UN, and generally acting as Pakistan's patron in the Muslim world. India didn't protest louder. It didn't issue démarches. It walked into Turkey's backyard and shook hands with the neighbour Turkey has been bullying for fifty years.

And here's what makes it brilliant: Turkey can't complain without sounding hypocritical. You want to arm Pakistan against India? Then India gets to arm Cyprus against Turkey. You want to question sovereignty? Turkey occupies a third of an EU member state. India supports that member state's territorial integrity.

The quiet part, said out loud: **India is building a network of partnerships designed to make it progressively more expensive for any nation to arm Pakistan.** Engage with Pakistan's military adventurism, and you'll find Indian weapons systems in the hands of your regional rivals.

This isn't just the Cyprus playbook. Watch for similar moves with Greece, with Armenia, with any nation that has a grievance with a Pakistan-aligned state.

The era of India playing defence is over.
