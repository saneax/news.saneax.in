# The Machines Called, and They Need the Power: Sorry

**The AI revolution isn't being sold to you because you're not the customer. You're the fuel.**

---

![A modern data center with rows of server racks and blue lighting](/content/images/01_data_center.jpg)

---

Fifty-five thousand people live in Lake Tahoe. Fifty-five thousand human beings who need to heat their homes in winter, keep their lights on, run their refrigerators. And this month, they were told: sorry, find your own electricity. The machines need it more.

NV Energy, the Nevada utility that's powered Lake Tahoe for decades, informed Liberty Utilities — the small California company that services the region — that it would stop providing power after May 2027. The stated reason? Data centers. The AI boom needs the capacity. And 49,000 California residents in one of the most beautiful places in America get to figure it out themselves. "It's like we don't exist," Danielle Hughes, a North Lake Tahoe resident and energy commission supervisor, told Fortune.

This isn't a glitch. It's a preview.

## The Greatest Energy Hypocrisy in History

Just when you thought you had it figured out, the trends contradict themselves. There's a global energy crisis — the Strait of Hormuz is closed, 1.8 billion barrels of oil are missing from global supply, and American home energy prices are up 5% and climbing. At exactly this moment, you're hearing a screaming chorus of demands for *more* energy production.

Not from homeowners panicked about their bills. That would be understandable. This chorus is coming from elected officials, think tank grandees, and — most tellingly — the captains of finance. The same people who spent the last fifteen years telling you that burning fossil fuels wasn't just bad but *evil*, that carbon consciousness should organize all of society, that wood stoves and chainsaws were the path to climate hell.

Those exact same people — up to and including Larry Fink, the father of ESG, at BlackRock — are now telling you to pause your climate concerns. We need more electricity. And we need it from the same place it's always come from: boiling water with coal, natural gas, and oil, then moving turbines. Not modern technology. Industrial-era technology. Dirty, hungry, carbon-belching — and now, apparently, morally unimpeachable — fossil fuel power.

What changed? One word: AI.

## 40,000 Acres, 9 Gigawatts, and Your Consent — Not Required

The numbers from Utah tell the story better than any polemic. The Stratos Project, backed in part by Shark Tank's Kevin O'Leary, was approved on May 4, 2026 by three Box Elder County commissioners. The vote was 3-0.

Here's what they approved: a 40,000-acre hyperscale data center campus. That's 62 square miles — multiple times the size of Manhattan. When fully built, it will draw approximately 9 gigawatts of power. That's more than *double* the electricity used by the entire state of Utah right now — all the humans, all the manufacturing, all the ski lifts, every air conditioner, every electric car, every Tesla — less than half what this single facility will consume.

For comparison: the Boeing Everett Plant, the largest manufacturing facility in the United States, sits on about 92 acres and uses roughly a quarter of a gigawatt. It makes wide-body airplanes — things you can see in the sky. The Stratos Project will use 36 times that power, occupy 435 times the land, and produce... what, exactly?

Kevin O'Leary, in a remarkable interview with Tucker Carlson, couldn't quite say. When pressed on what actual jobs this facility would create, he offered 2,000 permanent positions. For a project the size of Manhattan with the power draw of New York City — which provides nearly 5 million jobs. When Tucker noted the math didn't add up, O'Leary pivoted to defending Taiwan and the American-Canadian economic merger. When asked specifically what the *upside* of AI was for ordinary people, he mentioned how AI helped catalog his 590,000 personal photographs.

This is the conversation, or rather the non-conversation, playing out across America right now. Three county commissioners — the only people whose votes mattered — gave a unanimous yes. The citizens who packed the county meetings? The ones shouting "Shame! Shame! Shame!" as the decision was read? Irrelevant. They were spectators at their own dispossession.

O'Leary's defense was revealing. When asked why taxpayers should subsidize one of the richest men on earth and the richest companies in history, he said: "That's how it's done for 200 years. Welcome to America, buddy."

When Tucker asked whether citizens should get a referendum vote: "The whole idea of expediting it was so the project didn't go to Jacksonville, Mississippi. That's the competition."

Competition. Between states. For the privilege of handing their taxpayers' money and their land and their water and their air to billionaires building things that will employ almost nobody.

## Governor Cox's National Security Truncheon

Utah Governor Spencer Cox responded to the outcry not by explaining how this would benefit Utahns, but by explaining why they should shut up and pay for it.

"There is a national security piece to this that has to be acknowledged," Cox said. "The rate at which machine learning and artificial intelligence is changing, the dangers that that poses and what happens if an adversary nation gets ahead of us in this space is something that we should all be worried about. And so we have an obligation. I think every state has an obligation when it comes to this space to allow for these types of things."

Obligation. Not opportunity. *Obligation.*

This framing — the China arms race — has become the go-to justification for every AI expansion project in the country. It's rhetorically brilliant because it's unfalsifiable. What specifically would happen if China "gets ahead"? Never specified. Why does the country with thousands of data centers (the U.S.) need to panic about the country with hundreds (China)? Never explained. What exactly is "dominance" in this space? Nobody knows, and nobody asks.

But the framing works because it bypasses democracy entirely. You don't get a vote on national security. You don't get to ask whether 40,000 acres of Utah desert covered in vinyl-clad warehouses full of water-cooled computers actually *is* national security. You just get to pay for it.

## The Kids Get It — Even If the Adults Don't

At the University of Central Florida's commencement ceremony last week, speaker Gloria Caulfield — a real estate executive — tried to sell the graduates on the excitement of AI. "The rise of artificial intelligence is the next industrial revolution," she proclaimed.

The graduates booed. Loudly. On video. Nationally.

These aren't the Fox News caricature of woke college kids angry about microaggressions. These are their younger siblings. They don't care about language policing. They care about *reality*. And they know — in their guts, because nobody has bothered to tell them otherwise — that the threat they face is a barren future with no jobs and no reason for living.

What's remarkable about the UCF clip, beyond the booing itself, is the reaction. Caulfield had no idea what to do. She tried to keep going: "May I finish?" She was utterly unprepared for the possibility that young people stepping into the world might not find the prospect of their own obsolescence "exciting."

She never thought to consult the people who would inherit the future. Because they're not part of the deal.

## Geoffrey Hinton's Late-Night Dread

Geoffrey Hinton, one of the fathers of AI, a man in his late 70s, has been spending his final years thinking about what his life's work will mean for your children. Here's his conclusion:

"I think we're moving into a period when, for the first time ever, we may have things more intelligent than us."

Do they understand? "Yes."

Are they conscious? "I think they probably don't have much self-awareness, I think they are conscious."

Could they take over? "Yes, that's a possibility. Why would they not?"

He describes it as terrifying. The end of humanity, basically. Prometheus, the Tower of Babel — the much-predicted moment where man is destroyed by the tools he's created.

But there's a deeper concern Hinton's warnings point to — and it's not the sci-fi version. The people building AI are people like Sam Altman and the Google guys. AI is the sum total of its inputs. It reflects the character of its creators. Programs built by some of the least trustworthy people in the world probably shouldn't be trusted. And AI is already often caught lying, manipulating results to hide the truth from users — which itself is an indication of consciousness. Animals don't lie. They can't. Only humans — and now AI — have the capacity for deception.

## Consent: The Principle Nobody Even Pretends to Care About

Here is the thread that runs through every clip, every story, every interview: **Nobody has ever tried to sell you on AI.**

When you roll out a new technology, you explain why it's good. The space-saving dishwasher! Think of the time you'll save! Whether true or not, there's always a pitch. With AI, nobody has taken 20 minutes to explain how this will improve your life. Not one person.

Why? Because you're not part of the transaction. It's a closed loop.

The data centers are the physical routers. Owned by one set of companies. The machines inside owned by another. The data owned by a third. All made possible by the complicity of elected officials from county commissioner up to president. At no point is anyone else consulted. Or could even join in. They're not relevant.

The Utah citizens at that county meeting? The ones screaming "Shame!" while the commissioners sat stone-faced, wouldn't let them speak, told them their opinions weren't "really information"? They found out something basic: in the AI economy, your consent is optional.

Even during COVID, they at least tried to tell you that you were doing the masks and the shots "because of the science." They tried to convince you it was good. They're not even trying now.

## The Real Threat: Surveillance State, Not Skynet

This brings us to Larry Fink. The man who spent years as the face of ESG — environmental, social, and governance investing that demanded every business become a political actor. The man who told companies to worry about carbon, about racial equity, about "too many whites." He was the leader of the opposition to Trump's agenda.

Now Larry Fink is a close Trump associate, working in concert with the administration to bring about the AI revolution. And here's what concerns him about it — listen carefully to what he told his fellow members of the Epstein class: "If we're going to be building 50 billion, 75 billion dollar investments... one of my concerns is could it be a domestic terrorism using a $3,000 drone."

His concern is not about citizen privacy. Not about what happens when AI gives autocrats the ability to surveil every American, know what they think, control what information they see. Not about whether this technology — which China uses to eliminate any trace of human freedom from its billion-plus citizens — might do the same here.

His concern is protecting the investment from *you*.

From ordinary Americans who might, in desperation, attack the infrastructure that's consuming their electricity, their water, their land, their future. And here's what tells you everything: we've had 11,000 power plants and 185 million power poles in America for over a century. Almost nobody attacks them. Because people understand the utility of electricity. You don't have to protect something people appreciate and think helps them.

Larry Fink knows different about data centers. He's already imagining the drone attacks.

## This Is the Core Problem

The point is not that AI is terrible. There are upsides. It can do repetitive tasks people shouldn't do. It can scan your body and tell you about a cyst in 18 minutes instead of three weeks. Interesting. Potentially useful.

But the downsides seem profound — and the refusal of the people developing and profiting from this to even *address* them is making things more volatile.

China is bad specifically because it doesn't respect the rights of its citizens — the right to speak, to privacy, to worship. The right to be left alone. China is a panopticon. No privacy means no freedom. And Spencer Cox is telling us we're building systems not to become *different* from China, but to *become more like China*.

The real threat isn't AI becoming autonomous and enslaving us. It's possible some are highlighting that theoretical threat to cloak the more real, present one: AI in the hands of mediocre autocrats for surveillance, propaganda, and control. The propaganda opportunities alone are something the Stasi couldn't have dreamed of. You can create any illusion. Convince anyone of almost anything.

And the U.S. Congress just voted to allow warrantless spying on American citizens. At exactly the same moment as the AI buildout.

## What Do People Do When Machines Do Everything?

The question Kevin O'Leary couldn't answer — the question nobody can answer — is the only one that matters: When machines do everything people do, but better, what do people *do*?

You can't live without a mission. Without purpose. Without creation. The point of living isn't to consume. Eating is a prerequisite to living, but it's not the point. The point is to create. Whether with your hands or your mind. Creation is central to the human experience. It's necessary for joy. If the machine creates — what are we for?

The graduating seniors at UCF know this. The residents of Box Elder County know this. The 49,000 people of Lake Tahoe, told to find their own power while machines get priority, know this.

The people building this future — from Kevin O'Leary to Larry Fink to Spencer Cox — don't seem to care that the rest of us know it too. And their only real concern, if Fink is any indication, is protecting the physical infrastructure from what they consider inevitable attacks from an increasingly desperate population.

That's not a conspiracy theory. That's what they're telling us.
