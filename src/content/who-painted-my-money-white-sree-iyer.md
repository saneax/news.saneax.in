# When Fiction Wears a Badge: Sree Iyer, Fake Currency, and the Book That Claims to Have Predicted It All

**By Arjun Mehra | May 18, 2026**

---

There's a video making the rounds on YouTube. Titled *"NIA's Biggest Crackdown Yet? A 6-State Plot Against Modi Govt Exposed,"* it has all the elements of a gripping investigative report: midnight raids, recovered documents, a shadowy conspiracy spanning six states, a plot to assassinate the Prime Minister.

But the video isn't journalism. It's a 12-minute monologue by Sree Iyer — Silicon Valley inventor, patent holder, founder of the right-leaning news site PGurus — promoting his 2019 novel **"Who Painted My Money White?"** And the pitch is simple: *I wrote about this years ago. Now it's happening. My fiction wasn't fiction.*

That's a hell of a marketing hook. The question is whether it holds up to scrutiny.

## The Book: A Roman à Clef Dressed as a Thriller

**"Who Painted My Money White?"** (self-published, 2019, 288 pages) is Book 1 of Sree Iyer's "Money Trilogy." The plot, in the author's own synopsis published on PGurus:

> A ship carrying containers of high-quality counterfeit Indian rupees — printed in Pakistan, each valued at ₹5,000 crore — arrives in Kochi. The fake currency, indistinguishable from the real thing, enters the banking system through cooperative banks and chartered accountants. Inflation spikes. Real estate booms. The RBI discovers that instead of 87% of demonetized notes returning to the system, 113% comes back. Meanwhile, Pakistan is printing Indian currency in three mints. There's honey-trapping of Finance Ministry officials in London. ISIS agents are involved. And there's a plan to assassinate the newly elected Prime Minister.

The book is transparently a **roman à clef** — a novel with a key. Every character maps to a real person:

| Fictional Character | Real-Life Counterpart |
|---|---|
| Finance Minister "Maida" | P. Chidambaram |
| PM "Dhillon" | Manmohan Singh |
| "Biplap Banerjee" | Pranab Mukherjee |
| "Dalpat Dalvi" | Sharad Pawar |
| "Maker Wirewala" | Robert Vadra |
| Party Supremo "Madam" | Sonia Gandhi |
| "Freedom Party" | Congress/UPA |
| "People's Voice" | BJP/NDA |
| The CM of Gujarat | Narendra Modi |

One reviewer on Goodreads noted wryly: *"No other work of fiction would require the warning more than this book that 'any resemblance of the characters to any person living or dead is purely coincidental.'"*

Iyer doesn't even try to disguise it. The "boldness," as one reviewer put it, is the point. The book reads less like a thriller and more like a fictionalized indictment of the UPA decade — a greatest-hits compilation of every corruption allegation from 2004-2014, strung together with a fake currency plot.

## The Video: Marketing Disguised as Investigation

The YouTube video at `7ePdzdUFmfg` is posted on ABP News but the format is unmistakably a PGurus production. Iyer speaks directly to camera, alternating between Hindi and English, referencing real NIA operations to create the impression that his fiction was prophetic.

Here's what he's actually doing:

**1. Collapsing multiple investigations into one narrative.** The video references the NIA's raids across six states, the ISIS Ballari module case, the PFI crackdown ("Operation Octopus"), the Phulwari Sharif conspiracy, and the PM Modi Patna rally threat — as if they're all connected. They're not. The Ballari module was 4 states, not 6. The PFI case is about domestic radicalization, not counterfeit currency. The Patna plot was a PFI training camp, not a Pakistani intelligence operation.

**2. Using the authority of real institutions to validate fiction.** The NIA is a real investigative agency that has done genuinely important work. The PFI was banned for documented reasons — links to ISIS, terrorist funding, targeted killings of Hindu activists. But Iyer's claim that his novel "predicted" these events is a classic propaganda technique: write about things that are already happening in some form, wait for the public to learn about them, then claim prescience.

**3. The feedback loop.** Iyer runs PGurus, which publishes right-leaning content. He writes novels that dramatize that content. He then appears on news channels to promote the novels by referencing the news that his own site helped amplify. It's a closed loop — fiction informs the editorial line, the editorial line feeds back into the fiction, and both are marketed as investigative journalism.

## The Real NIA Operations: Separating Fact from Iyer's Fiction

Let's be clear about what the NIA has actually uncovered, because it's significant enough without the fictional embellishment:

**Operation Octopus (Sep 2022):** Nationwide raids on PFI offices and members across 15+ states. The NIA's chargesheet alleges PFI members were recruiting youth for terrorist training, running weapons camps, and had prepared a document called "Mission 2047" outlining a plan to establish Islamic rule in India by the centenary of independence. The government banned PFI and 8 affiliated fronts under UAPA.

**Phulwari Sharif Case (Jul 2022):** NIA arrested PFI members from Patna who were allegedly planning attacks on "sensitive installations." The ED's remand note claimed one module was training specifically to target PM Modi's Patna rally.

**The Funding Trail:** ED investigations traced ₹120+ crore in suspicious deposits — mostly cash from unknown sources, some routed through Gulf countries. Properties worth ₹74 crore were attached.

**The ISIS Ballari Module:** Separate from PFI — a Karnataka-based cell inspired by ISIS, planning attacks on judiciary and police targets. 8 arrests across 4 states.

These are serious investigations with documented evidence. They don't need a novelist to make them important. But they also don't prove that Pakistan is printing Indian currency in three mints, or that honey-trapped officials leaked security features, or that 113% of demonetized notes returned to the RBI.

Those are plot points from a work of fiction.

## Who Is Sree Iyer?

The author is genuinely accomplished in his primary field. He holds **37 US patents** — mostly in medical devices, RFID technology, and semiconductor design. He worked at Intel and other Silicon Valley firms. He founded a company called **VisaAcquirer** — an interesting name given his fixation on payment systems.

In 2014, he founded **PGurus**, a news and opinion site that positions itself as an alternative to mainstream media. The site has faced credibility challenges:

- **Fact-checking organizations** have flagged PGurus for publishing misleading claims, including doctored videos and out-of-context quotes
- The site has been accused of running **communal and Islamophobic content** — a charge Iyer would likely dismiss as "liberal bias"
- PGurus operates in the same ecosystem as **OpIndia, Swarajya, and Republic TV** — the post-2014 right-wing digital media infrastructure

Iyer's worldview is consistent: he believes the UPA years were a decade of institutional corruption enabled by a pliant media, that demonetization was a necessary surgical strike on black money and counterfeit currency, and that Islamic radicalization is an existential threat to India that the "liberal establishment" refuses to acknowledge.

If you share that worldview, his books will feel like vindication. If you don't, they'll read like propaganda with footnotes.

## The Critical Reception

The book has a **3.72/5** rating on Goodreads from 81 ratings and 24 reviews — modest numbers for a "thriller." The reviews reveal a pattern:

**The enthusiasts** (4-5 stars) praise the book's "boldness" and "eye-opening" revelations. They don't evaluate it as literature — they evaluate it as a political document. One reviewer wrote: *"I feel that this book is one which needs to be read by all Indians, especially those who have endured those despairing days of UPA 2."*

**The skeptics** (2-3 stars) acknowledge the ambition but criticize the execution. The most common complaints:

1. **It reads like a news report, not a novel.** *"Author spends a lot of page space for the events that we already know, rather than the fiction part of the story."*
2. **Characters are cardboard.** *"The characters who populate the pages haven't been developed well."*
3. **The thriller elements feel forced.** *"The assassination attempt is not dramatic enough."*
4. **Plot holes in the conspiracy.** *"Instead of manufacturing weapons in India, why doesn't Pakistan simply smuggle them?"*

A reviewer on Goodreads summarized it well: *"At various points in the narrative, the events are explained in a tad too simplistic a manner leading to important national events appearing to be frivolous and devoid of the gravitas that they deserve."*

## The Core Problem: When the Genre Is Both Shield and Sword

Here's the fundamental issue with evaluating a book like "Who Painted My Money White?":

If you criticize it as **fiction**, the defense is: *"But it's based on real events! Look at what the NIA found!"*

If you criticize it as **non-fiction**, the defense is: *"But it's fiction! The disclaimer is right there!"*

This is the **genre-as-shield** maneuver. By occupying the liminal space between fiction and journalism, Iyer can claim the authority of both while accepting the accountability of neither. The characters are fake, so he can't be sued for libel. But the events are "real," so the book markets itself as an exposé.

This isn't unique to Iyer. It's a growing genre in Indian publishing — the "fictionalized truth" that functions as political propaganda while hiding behind the fig leaf of creative license. Vivek Agnihotri's "Urban Naxals" played the same game. Anand Ranganathan's thrillers operate in this space. It's effective precisely because it short-circuits the normal critical frameworks we use to evaluate information.

## The Data: What We Actually Know About Fake Currency in India

Since Iyer's book makes specific claims about counterfeit currency, let's look at the actual data:

**RBI Annual Reports on Fake Indian Currency Notes (FICN):**

| Year | Fake ₹500 notes detected | Fake ₹1000 notes detected | Total FICN detected |
|------|-------------------------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| 2016-17 | 199,946 | 152,743 | 522,783 |
| 2017-18 | 9,892 | 15,231 | 270,726 |
| 2018-19 | 15,779 | 1,258 | 217,289 |
| 2019-20 | 20,576 | 414 | 246,199 |
| 2020-21 | 22,588 | 190 | 164,504 |
| 2021-22 | 35,474 | 73 | 175,311 |
| 2022-23 | 48,608 | N/A | 225,769 |

The jump in fake ₹500 notes (from 9,892 in 2017-18 to 48,608 in 2022-23) is a **392% increase** — and Iyer's thesis depends on using this statistic to argue that demonetization failed and counterfeiters adapted to the new notes.

But here's the context that Iyer omits:

- **Total currency in circulation** grew from ₹18 lakh crore (2016) to ₹35 lakh crore (2023) — the detection rate as a percentage of total currency actually dropped
- The ₹500 note became the highest-denomination currency bill, so counterfeiters naturally shifted to it
- The total FICN detected actually **declined 68%** from the 2016-17 peak — even with the recent ₹500 increase
- Counterfeit detection is a function of both criminal activity AND enforcement — higher numbers can mean better policing, not just more crime

This is the problem with extracting statistics to fit a predetermined narrative. You can make the numbers say almost anything if you choose your timeframe and your framing carefully.

## The Verdict

"Who Painted My Money White?" is many things at once: a political thriller, a roman à clef, a prosecution brief against the UPA, a marketing vehicle for PGurus, and now — through the ABP News video — a claim to prophetic insight about national security threats.

As a political document, it's interesting. As a thriller, it's mediocre. As journalism, it's unreliable. As marketing, it's brilliant.

Sree Iyer has figured out something important: in an era of collapsing trust in institutions, the boundary between fiction and non-fiction is a commercial opportunity. Write a novel that "exposes" what you believe to be true. Wait for real events to provide superficial confirmation. Then market the book as having "predicted" those events. The algorithm rewards it. The audience — already primed to distrust mainstream sources — embraces it. And the critics can't touch you because, after all, it's just fiction.

The NIA doesn't need Sree Iyer's novels to justify its work. The PFI was banned on evidence, not imagination. Counterterrorism operations succeed or fail on intelligence, not plot twists. And Indians deserve to understand their country's security challenges through facts and analysis — not through thrillers pretending to be investigations, or investigations pretending to be thrillers.

As for the video — watch it if you're curious about how the propaganda feedback loop operates in real time. Just don't confuse marketing with journalism, or a self-published novel with a national security briefing.
