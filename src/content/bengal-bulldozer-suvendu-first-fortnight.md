# Bulldozers, Border Fences, and a Broken System: Suvendu Adhikari's First 14 Days

**By Arjun Mehra | May 18, 2026**

---

The man who buried Mamata Banerjee's 15-year empire now has to build something on top of the rubble.

Suvendu Adhikari — former TMC general, the man who first defeated Didi in Nandigram by 1,956 votes in 2021, then routed her by over 15,000 in Bhabanipur in 2026 — has been Bengal's Chief Minister for barely a month. And he's moving like a man who knows exactly how short his honeymoon will be.

![AI-generated illustration: A cargo route map stretching across Asia representing trade corridors and geopolitical strategy](/content/images-borders-bullets/01_trade_route_ships.png)

The first cabinet meeting in Nabanna produced six headline decisions that sent a clear signal: the era of TMC's parallel state is over. But a deeper analysis of the government's opening moves — captured in extraordinary granular detail by commentators tracking every decision — reveals something far more ambitious and far more dangerous: an attempt to rebuild the institutional architecture of West Bengal from the ground up.

## The Six That Made Headlines

The first cabinet meeting's six decisions were calibrated for maximum symbolic impact:

**1. Ayushman Bharat implementation.** Bengal was one of the last states holding out against the Centre's flagship health insurance scheme — not because it was bad policy, but because accepting it would mean acknowledging Delhi's authority. Adhikari's first act was to say: we're done with performative federalism. The scheme covers 10 crore Indians with Rs 5 lakh annual coverage. Bengal's exclusion was always ideological, not practical.

**2. BSF land transfer for border fencing — 45-day deadline.** For 15 years, the TMC government stonewalled BSF requests for land to erect fencing on the Bangladesh border. The reasons were never stated officially but widely understood: the porous border was economically and politically useful. Cattle smuggling, illegal infiltration, and cross-border criminal networks flourished. Adhikari gave 45 days. The message to Dhaka was unmistakable.

**3. Central schemes integration.** PM Awas Yojana, PM Kisan Samman Nidhi, Jal Jeevan Mission — all blocked or bottlenecked under TMC rule as acts of political theater against the Modi government. Now they're being fast-tracked.

**4. Caste census announcement.** A direct copy of Bihar's template, and a signal to Bengal's politically dormant OBC and Dalit populations that the BJP's social engineering playbook is coming to the state. This one has long-term electoral implications that TMC strategists should be losing sleep over.

**5. Age relaxation for government jobs.** The upper age limit raised by two years — a sop to the youth demographic that booed AI cheerleaders at commencements worldwide. Bengal's educated unemployed are a ticking political bomb. This kicks the fuse down the road.

**6. New criminal laws implementation.** Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) replacing the colonial IPC. More than a name change — it signals alignment with the Centre's decolonization project that TMC had reflexively opposed.

## The Ones Nobody's Talking About

But the six cabinet decisions are the tame part. A detailed analysis of the government's first fortnight — drawing from official briefings, police headquarters meetings, and administrative orders — reveals a much broader agenda that's being implemented at breakneck speed.

### The Reckoning: Post-Poll Violence Files Reopened

This is the big one, and it's going to get ugly before it gets clean.

Adhikari has ordered the reopening of **all post-poll violence cases from 2021 and 2023**. The 2021 Assembly elections were followed by what can only be described as pogroms against BJP voters — homes burned, businesses destroyed, women raped, men beaten to death. 321 BJP karyakartas killed according to party records. The 2023 panchayat elections saw a repeat performance, though smaller in scale.

The Indian Express reported on May 14 that **300 post-poll violence cases from 2021** are now "under lens" — with particular focus on crimes against women. The Telegraph India confirmed Suvendu's first meeting with police leadership focused on reopening these files. The CBI had already arrested the prime accused in one BJP worker's murder after a 4-year manhunt — but hundreds of cases were buried, witnesses intimidated, evidence destroyed.

The question now is: who investigates the investigators? The West Bengal Police were complicit. Top IPS officers watched the violence unfold and did nothing — or worse, facilitated it. Adhikari is calling them out publicly, asking what senior IPS officers "actually did" during the violence. The answer, in most cases, is: whatever the political masters demanded.

### The Border Doctrine

The BSF land transfer is just the beginning. The new government is pushing for a fundamental redefinition of the Bangladesh border's "rules of engagement" — demanding parity with the Pakistan border. This means shoot-to-kill authority against infiltrators and smugglers, electronic surveillance, and an end to the de facto open border that has transformed Bengal's demographics over three decades.

Cattle smuggling across the Bangladesh border is a multibillion-rupee parallel economy. It funds radical organizations. It creates a nexus of corrupt officials, politicians, and cross-border criminal networks. The new government has launched a crackdown — but as any observer of Indian border politics knows, smuggling syndicates don't die from one police action. They adapt. The test is whether the crackdown has institutional staying power, or whether it fizzles when the political costs mount.

### Coal, Sand, and the Mining Syndicates

Illegal coal mining in Bengal's Raniganj-Asansol belt is an environmental catastrophe and a political economy unto itself. Unregulated mines cause land subsidence — entire regions literally sinking into the ground. Sand mining destroys river ecosystems. Both are controlled by politically-protected syndicates that funnel money to party coffers.

Adhikari has announced a crackdown on illegal mining. But here's the uncomfortable truth: these syndicates were created by the political class. Many of the same people now tasked with dismantling them were their protectors. The new government will need to show it's serious by prosecuting the kingpins, not just the foot soldiers.

### The Loudspeaker Doctrine

One of the most quietly radical changes: enforcement of Supreme Court guidelines on loudspeakers at religious places. Street prayers that block roads — common across Bengal during religious festivals — are now restricted, particularly near the Eastern Command headquarters on Red Road. Stone-pelting incidents will face "action." The subtext is clear: the new government will not tolerate public order being held hostage by religious sentiment.

This is classic BJP governance — the muscular Hindu nationalism that its base demands — but applied with a lawyerly precision. No bans on religious practice. Just enforcement of existing Court guidelines that the previous government conveniently ignored.

### The Bureaucracy Purge

The most dramatic move: **senior IPS and IAS officers sent for mandatory central training**. This isn't routine. It's a purge dressed in the language of professional development.

For 15 years, West Bengal's civil service was systematically politicized. Officers who showed independence were transferred to punishment postings. Those who played ball were rewarded with plum assignments and post-retirement sinecures. The TMC built a parallel command structure where party functionaries overruled district magistrates and SPs.

Adhikari is sending a message: the party is no longer above the administration. Senior bureaucrats who were "re-contracted" after retirement — a TMC specialty — have been told to leave. Several have reportedly resigned rather than face scrutiny.

The risk here is real. A demoralized, politicized bureaucracy that's had its protectors removed can become paralyzed — neither loyal to the new government nor willing to do its job. Rebuilding institutional capacity takes years. Adhikari has months before the public stops blaming TMC and starts blaming him.

## The Structural Reforms Nobody Wants to Touch

Beyond the headline-grabbing actions, the new government has identified a set of structural reforms that are genuinely ambitious — and genuinely difficult:

**The Rent Control Act.** Bengal's urban rental market is frozen in amber by a 1950s-era law that caps rents at absurdly low levels. Landlords can't evict non-paying tenants. Properties can't be redeveloped. The result: Kolkata's magnificent colonial architecture is literally crumbling because no one can make the economics of restoration work.

Reforming rent control is political suicide. Tenants are voters. But without reform, Kolkata's housing stock will continue to decay and new construction will remain uneconomical. The Adhikari government has signaled it wants to tackle this — but whether it has the political courage to actually do it remains to be seen.

**Urban Land Ceiling Act.** Another colonial-era zombie law that makes large-scale real estate development nearly impossible in Bengal. Combined with rent control, it has created a housing market that serves neither landlords nor tenants — just lawyers and brokers.

**Police Capacity Building.** Bengal has roughly 100 police personnel per 100,000 population. Uttar Pradesh — with similar population density challenges — has nearly 4 lakh police after sustained capacity building under the Yogi government. The new government estimates Bengal needs a complete overhaul: 3-4 lakh professionally trained police. That's a decade-long project.

**The North-South Divide.** Bengal's development has been overwhelmingly Calcutta-centric. The northern districts — Siliguri, Jalpaiguri, the Dooars — have been systematically neglected. The new government is talking about redirecting investment northward. But talk is cheap. The test is whether North Bengal actually sees factories, logistics hubs, and defense manufacturing facilities materialize.

**Industrial Policy.** Bengal doesn't have one. The TMC years produced no major industrial investment outside the services sector. The state's manufacturing GDP share is among the lowest of India's major states. Adhikari's team knows this is the core economic problem — and the one that matters most to the youth who will either make or break his government in 2031.

## The Contradiction at the Heart of This Project

Here's what makes this fascinating to watch from an analytical distance:

Suvendu Adhikari is trying to do in West Bengal what Narendra Modi did in Gujarat — use a massive electoral mandate to restructure the state's institutional architecture. But the circumstances are fundamentally different.

Modi had 13 years in Gujarat. Adhikari likely has 5 years — and he knows it. The BJP's 207-seat mandate was built on a protest vote against Mamata, not a positive affirmation of BJP governance. The Muslim population — 27% of Bengal's electorate — voted almost entirely against the BJP. The consolidation that gave Adhikari office could fragment just as quickly.

The speed of the reforms reflects this political calculus. Adhikari's team is burning through its agenda at a pace that suggests they know the window is narrow. Every decision is being made in the first 100 days because after that, governing gets hard. Scandals erupt. Coalitions crack. By-elections go wrong.

But speed has a cost. Reopening post-poll violence cases without first restructuring the police force that was complicit in those crimes is like asking the arsonist to investigate the fire. The border reforms will work only if the Centre delivers on its side — and Delhi's attention span for state-level problems is notoriously short. The bureaucratic purge could leave a vacuum that criminal elements are only too happy to fill.

## The Verdict So Far

There's no question that something has fundamentally changed in West Bengal. The terror that TMC's parallel state inflicted on civil society — the fear of speaking out, the certainty that the state would protect party goons, the resignation to a system where law meant nothing and political connections meant everything — that fear is visibly receding.

But fear's departure leaves a void. What fills it?

Adhikari's first decisions are genuinely bold. The post-poll violence review, the border reforms, the mining crackdown — these are not cosmetic changes. They target the deep structures of TMC's power: the system of organized violence, the cross-border patronage networks, the illegal resource extraction that funded the political machine.

The question is whether they survive contact with reality.

Every one of these reforms creates enemies. The police union will resist accountability. The smuggling syndicates will fight back — with money, violence, and political patronage. The politicized bureaucracy will drag its feet. The TMC, even in opposition, retains enormous institutional memory and street-level muscle. And the Centre's enthusiasm for backing a state government tends to cool when the next national crisis demands attention.

Suvendu Adhikari has made a strong first impression. But the real test isn't the first cabinet meeting. It's the first crisis. The first by-election loss. The first scandal involving a BJP functionary. The first moment when the structural resistance to reform reveals itself.

That's when we'll know whether Bengal's saffron dawn was a permanent sunrise, or just a brief and beautiful morning.
