# The Man Washington Can't Stop Watching

So there's this guy. Former Fox News host. Fired in 2023. Everyone said he was done. Finished. A relic of the cable news era.

Tucker Carlson is now the most dangerous man in American media. Not because he has a network behind him — he doesn't. Not because he has political power — he's never held office. He's dangerous because he keeps asking questions that the people in charge don't want to answer. And millions of people are listening.

On a Saturday in early May 2026, Carlson sat down with The New York Times — the newspaper he's spent years criticising — for what became one of the most talked-about interviews of the year. The video has been circulating with a title that tells you everything: "This Tucker Carlson Interview Has Washington Nervous."

Why would a podcast interview make the most powerful city in the world nervous? Let's look at what he actually said.

## What He Said

Carlson told the Times something remarkable for a man who spent a decade as Trump's most prominent media defender: he was wrong. "I spent 10 years defending Trump on Fox News," he said. "I'd probably do it again, because on the issues I agree with him… I told people this guy will keep us out of the next Iraq, specifically will keep us out of a regime-change war with Iran… And here we are in the middle of a regime-change war in Iran… so I'm sorry."

That's not normal. Public figures, especially media figures, don't admit they were wrong. They pivot. They reframe. They memory-hole their own predictions. Carlson said the quiet part out loud: he sold Trump as the anti-war candidate, and Trump launched a war.

Then it got stranger.

When the Times confronted him about previously asking whether Trump could be the "Antichrist," Carlson denied ever saying it — and the reporter played the audio. Carlson's response: "I don't know where that comes from… I may have said some are asking that."

The moment was awkward. The kind of awkward that goes viral. Mediaite called it "the reporter brought receipts." Yahoo ran with "Tucker Carlson Cornered." Across the political spectrum, the clip spread.

But here's what's interesting. Nobody's talking about the Antichrist gaffe except the media. Regular people — the ones commenting on YouTube, on X, in the forums — are talking about something else entirely.

## What People Are Actually Discussing

Scroll through the comments. Not the blue-check takes. The real ones.

"The man admitted he was wrong about a war. When was the last time you saw that?"

"Whether you like him or not, he's the only one saying what half the country is thinking."

"CIA reading his texts? And we're supposed to pretend that's normal?"

There's a pattern. People aren't debating whether Carlson called Trump the Antichrist. They're asking why a journalist's private texts were being read by an intelligence agency. They're asking why, exactly four years after the Iraq war everyone was promised wouldn't happen, America is in another Middle Eastern conflict. They're asking who decides which voices are acceptable and which need to be "framed for a crime."

## The Intelligence Agency Question

In March, Carlson posted a video that stopped just short of paranoid — except it might not be paranoia. He alleged the CIA had been reading his private texts with Iranian contacts, and that the Justice Department was preparing to charge him as a "foreign agent."

"I have never once been an agent of a foreign government," Carlson said. "I have spoken to foreign governments, yes. That's what journalists do."

The CIA and DOJ haven't commented. That silence is louder than any denial. Newsweek covered it. The Hindustan Times in India covered it. The London Times covered it. A story about an American journalist being monitored by his own government went global — because it raises a question that applies everywhere: can a government decide who's a journalist and who's a "foreign agent" based on which foreign governments they talk to?

## The Ted Cruz Feud

Then there's the Ted Cruz situation. Carlson said the Texas senator is "more morally repulsive" than Nick Fuentes — the far-right commentator whose interview Carlson now says he regrets doing. USA Today covered the escalating feud on May 5.

This is the strange position Carlson occupies now: too liberal for the far-right (regrets the Fuentes interview), too conservative for the establishment (questioning the Iran war), and too unpredictable for everyone. He's burning bridges in all directions.

## What We Know

- **Carlson has broken with Trump** over the Iran war, publicly apologising to his audience for his previous defense of the president on foreign policy.
- **The CIA allegations remain unaddressed** — no confirmation, no denial, no investigation announced. The story has simply... hung there since March.
- **His audience has not left him.** The YouTube numbers, the podcast downloads, the engagement — all suggest a viewership that follows him *because* he's unpredictable, not despite it.
- **The NYT interview was a strategic choice** — he went into the lion's den of legacy media knowing they'd try to trip him up. He got tripped up on the Antichrist quote. He won on the Iran admission. Both sides claimed victory.

## What We Don't Know

- **Is the DOJ actually pursuing charges?** Carlson says yes. No one in government has confirmed or denied.
- **Did the CIA actually read his texts?** The allegation is specific. The response has been silence.
- **What happens to the anti-war right after Trump?** Carlson told Megyn Kelly on May 7 that Trump "will be gone relatively soon, and we'll still have this country." He's asking the question nobody in Republican politics wants to answer: what comes next?
- **Is Carlson building something, or burning everything?** The book imprint, the digital media empire, the refusal to align with any faction — it looks like a strategy. But with Carlson, you genuinely can't tell if it's three-dimensional chess or just a man who's stopped caring what anyone thinks.

## The Question That Remains

Why would a podcast interview make Washington nervous?

Maybe it's not the interview. Maybe it's what the interview represents: a man with an audience of millions, no boss, no network, no party, and no apparent fear of consequences — at a moment when the institutions he's questioning are less trusted than they've been in generations.

If you're in Washington, that's not a media story. That's a problem.
