# AI Wasn't Built For You: Scott Galloway and the Psychology of a World That Doesn't Need You

**Scott Galloway sat in a chair and said out loud what every tech CEO is thinking: they don't need you anymore. The question is what happens to a civilization when its people are told they're surplus — and what it reveals about the people doing the telling.**

*By Arjun Mehra*

## I. The Prophet of Redundancy

Scott Galloway — NYU professor, serial entrepreneur, and the man who has made a second career out of saying uncomfortable things in a loud voice — sat down with Steven Bartlett on *The Diary of a CEO* and delivered what amounts to a psychological unmasking of the AI age. The title of the episode was blunt: "AI Wasn't Built For You. The Rich Don't Need You Anymore."

But the real story wasn't in the headline. It was in the layers underneath — in the moment Galloway described a billion-dollar class of people who have literally planned their escape from civilization, in his clinical dissection of what happens when CEOs are mistaken for saviours, and in the raw, unguarded passage at the end when a man who has spent decades accumulating wealth broke down talking about his children.

The podcast is ostensibly about AI and economics. In reality, it's about what happens to the human mind — both the minds that build these systems and the minds that will be displaced by them — when economic value and human value become indistinguishable.

---

## II. The Core Arguments: A Map of the Dystopia

Galloway's thesis has several interlocking claims. Let us lay them out before we take them apart:

**1. Catastrophizing as a marketing strategy.** Tech CEOs — Sam Altman chief among them — have discovered that the more apocalyptic their warnings about AI, the higher their valuations climb. "We might destroy humanity" is, translated from Silicon Valley-speak, "Please value my company at $300 billion."

**2. The idolatry of innovators.** Galloway argues that as religion declined in the West, people didn't stop needing gods — they just found new ones. Steve Jobs was the first tech messiah. Now Altman, Dario Amodei, and the rest play the role of prophets who must be trusted rather than regulated. Galloway's warning: they are not your saviours. They are capitalist actors doing exactly what capitalist actors do.

**3. AI as labour replacement, not labour augmentation.** The now-famous anecdote about "Molly" — a single analyst who replaced five people using AI agents — isn't presented as productivity gain. It's presented as a harbinger. One Molly per company. Millions fewer jobs. And not in some distant future. Now.

**4. The nihilism of the 0.1%.** The most striking claim: roughly one in three American billionaires have what Galloway calls "go bags" — detailed escape plans involving private jets, New Zealand bunkers, and the assumption that the society they helped dismantle won't be worth staying for.

**5. The great decoupling.** The ultra-wealthy have seceded from shared American life. They don't fly commercial. They don't use public healthcare. Their children don't attend public schools. And because they experience none of the consequences of institutional decay, they have zero incentive to invest in fixing it.

**6. Sam Altman as Darth Vader.** The "villain's journey" in tech always follows the same arc: compelling visionary → beloved figure → discovery that they've been optimizing for shareholder value all along. Altman's arc, Galloway argues, is merely the latest and fastest.

## III. The Psychology of the Builders: Why the 0.1% Don't Care

Here is where the podcast transcends economics and enters the territory of moral psychology.

Galloway describes a conversation with a billionaire who outlined his "go plan" in meticulous detail — the pilots, the airport, the bunker in New Zealand. Galloway's response was devastating in its simplicity: "You don't think your pilots are going to kill you and your wife? You don't think the people in New Zealand are going to come take the rich guy's stuff?"

This is not merely a logistical critique. It is a psychological one. What Galloway is describing is *cognitive isolation* — a state so extreme that it produces its own reality distortion field. When you are surrounded exclusively by people who serve you, agree with you, and depend on you, you lose the capacity for adversarial reasoning. You cannot model how other humans will behave when the systems that protect your privilege collapse.

The psychological literature on this is extensive, though rarely applied to the ultra-wealthy. Researchers studying *power and reduced empathy* — notably Dacher Keltner at UC Berkeley — have demonstrated that individuals who hold power over others become progressively worse at reading other people's emotions, imagining their perspectives, and predicting their behaviour. Power, in the psychological sense, literally narrows your field of social vision.

The billionaire with the go bag isn't stupid. He is cognitively imprisoned. His wealth has constructed an environment so perfectly tailored to his comfort that he has lost the ability to imagine a world in which his comfort is irrelevant. He cannot conceive that the pilot might have loyalties beyond a paycheck — loyalties to his own family, his own survival, his own anger at the man who flew away while the world burned.

### The Nihilism Paradox

Galloway calls this nihilism. But it is a peculiar kind — *optimistic nihilism*, if you will. These individuals believe that the systems they are building might end civilization, but they believe they will survive the ending. They have enough money to opt out of consequences. This is not the nihilism of despair. It is the nihilism of *privilege so extreme that consequences have been outsourced*.

There is a term from environmental psychology that fits: *solastalgia* — the distress produced by environmental change impacting people while they are directly connected to their home environment. The billionaires feel no solastalgia because they have no home. They have compounds. They have bunkers. They have jets. But they have no *place* — no community whose suffering they experience as their own.

Yuval Noah Harari, in *Homo Deus*, described the emergence of what he called a "useless class" — billions of people who would be economically irrelevant but kept pacified with bread and circuses (or, in the modern version, algorithmic content and Universal Basic Income). What Galloway adds to this picture is the psychology of the *other side* — the people who will create this useless class and feel nothing about it. Not because they are evil, but because they have been cognitively insulated from the consequences.

## IV. The Psychology of the Displaced: What Happens When You're Surplus

The harder — and more important — question is what happens to the people who are told, implicitly or explicitly, that they are no longer economically necessary.

Galloway's advice is pragmatic: learn AI, use a "second screen," become the person who operates the machine rather than the person replaced by it. This is sound counsel for the individual. But it is worthless as civilizational analysis, and Galloway knows it. He says, almost in passing, that "a lot of people will find themselves on the wrong side of the trade." What he does not fully explore is the psychological devastation of being on that side.

### The Identity Crisis

Decades of psychological research have established that unemployment is not merely an economic event — it is an *identity catastrophe*. Marie Jahoda, Lazarsfeld, and Zeisel's classic study of Marienthal — an Austrian town that lost its factory in the 1930s — documented what happens when an entire community becomes economically superfluous. The residents didn't just lose income. They lost their sense of time, their social rhythms, their capacity for future planning. They became, in Jahoda's term, *resigned* — not depressed in the clinical sense, but emptied of expectation.

The psychologist Marie Jahoda identified five "latent functions" of employment beyond income: time structure, social contact, collective purpose, status and identity, and regular activity. Remove employment and you do not merely remove money. You remove the scaffolding of psychological existence.

Now project this forward. AI does not threaten one factory town. It threatens the cognitive labour of an entire civilization. The analysts, the lawyers, the executive assistants, the writers, the coders — the entire middle class of knowledge work that emerged in the late twentieth century as the successor to industrial labour. What Marienthal was to the industrial age, the AI displacement could be to the information age — but at a scale that dwarfs comparison.

### The Surplus Human and the Meaning Vacuum

Viktor Frankl, the psychiatrist and Holocaust survivor, argued that the primary human drive is not pleasure (Freud) or power (Adler) but *meaning* — the search for purpose and significance. Frankl's insight, born in the most extreme conditions imaginable, has been validated by subsequent research: people who perceive their lives as meaningful show greater resilience, better health outcomes, and stronger social bonds.

But what happens when the primary mechanism for meaning-making in modern capitalist societies — productive employment — is systematically removed? Not because of a recession that will end, but because of a structural shift that will not?

The answer, based on everything we know about human psychology, is not pretty. We already see previews: the opioid crisis in deindustrialized America was not merely a drug problem. It was a meaning problem. Communities that lost their economic purpose turned to substances that, at least temporarily, filled the void. The surge in "deaths of despair" — the term coined by economists Anne Case and Angus Deaton to describe rising mortality from suicide, drug overdose, and alcohol-related liver disease among middle-aged white Americans without college degrees — tracks almost perfectly with the collapse of stable employment in those demographics.

AI threatens to do to the college-educated middle class what globalization did to the working class. And the psychological infrastructure to handle that displacement does not exist.

## V. The Dystopian Parallels: What Literature Already Told Us

Galloway's podcast reads like a synthesis of every dystopian novel you were forced to read in school and probably dismissed as exaggeration.

### Brave New World — Huxley (1932)

Aldous Huxley imagined a world where the masses were kept docile not through force but through pleasure — *soma*, the feel-good drug that erased discontent. Galloway's description of AI as a kind of secular religion — "we're sending a question into the ether, thinking it's smarter than us, and it comes back with an empathetic, loving, correct answer" — is pure Huxley. The AI chatbot as soma. Comfortable. Responsive. And ultimately a substitute for genuine human connection and political agency.

### 1984 — Orwell (1949)

Orwell's vision was about information control. Galloway's concern about the coarsening of public discourse, the weaponization of platforms, and the radicalization of young men through algorithmic content curation is Orwellian in nature. But the mechanism has evolved. Orwell's Ministry of Truth controlled information by suppressing it. Our platforms control information by *overwhelming* us with it — drowning signal in noise until the very concept of a shared reality becomes untenable.

### The "Useless Class" — Harari (2017)

Harari's most provocative contribution to this discourse is his concept of the "useless class" — people who are not merely unemployed but *unemployable*, because the skills they possess have been permanently devalued by technology. Galloway's Molly anecdote is a case study in this: one person with AI agents doing the work of five. If scaled across the economy, the implication is that millions of knowledge workers will join a class that has no economic function — and, by the logic of market capitalism, no economic value.

### Player Piano — Vonnegut (1952)

The most prescient of all dystopian novels for the AI age is arguably Kurt Vonnegut's first novel, *Player Piano*, which imagined an America where machines have replaced most human labour. The engineers and managers who remain live in privileged enclaves. The displaced masses live in homogenous housing, perform make-work jobs, and seethe with resentment. Vonnegut, writing seventy-four years ago, described almost exactly the world Galloway is warning about: a society split between those who control the machines and those made irrelevant by them, held together by the thinnest of social contracts.

### The Eloi and the Morlocks — H.G. Wells (1895)

In *The Time Machine*, Wells imagined a future where humanity has split into two species: the beautiful, passive Eloi who live above ground, and the monstrous Morlocks who live below and tend the machinery. The twist — the Morlocks eat the Eloi. Galloway's observation that the billionaire with the go bag hasn't considered that his pilot might kill him is a Wellsian insight. The servants of the ultra-rich are human beings with their own agency. When the system breaks, the hierarchy does not hold.

## VI. The Anakin Skywalker Arc: Why We Keep Being Disappointed

Galloway's most psychologically acute observation may be his framing of the "villain's journey" in tech. The pattern: a compelling founder emerges. We project our hopes onto them. We decide they are "the good one." Then we discover they have been doing their job all along — optimizing for shareholder value, lobbying against regulation, and treating externalities as someone else's problem.

This is not a technology problem. It is a *projection* problem.

The psychologist Carl Jung described the process of *projection* — the tendency to attribute our own unacknowledged qualities onto others. When a society that feels powerless, anxious, and overwhelmed encounters a charismatic figure who appears to understand complex systems and promise a better future, it does not see the actual person. It sees a screen onto which it projects its own need for salvation.

The "hero's journey" from Anakin Skywalker to Darth Vader is not a story about evil. It is a story about *disappointed projection*. We made Altman a hero because we needed one. We made Zuckerberg a villain because we couldn't accept that we had made him a hero. The cycle repeats — Dario Amodei is currently being cast as "the good one" — because the psychological need is constant, and the actual behaviour of tech CEOs is remarkably consistent.

Galloway's solution — regulation, not trust — is both practically correct and psychologically naive. Regulation requires political will. Political will requires a functioning democracy. And a functioning democracy requires a citizenry that is not exhausted, overwhelmed, and drowning in algorithmic content designed to make them angry rather than informed.

## VII. The Mother at the End: Galloway's Own Unmasking

The most revealing moment in the entire two-hour conversation comes at the end, when Galloway is asked about his greatest setback. He answers without hesitation: "My mother dying. And you can never tell your parents how much you love them too much."

He breaks down. Not performative emotion — the real thing. A 61-year-old man who has spent the conversation talking about market caps, AI agents, and the nihilism of billionaires is suddenly a boy who misses his mother. The interviewer, Steven Bartlett, notices: "I see so much emotion in you when you talk about it. Your eyes filled with tears."

Galloway's response is worth quoting at length: "I hope my boys feel the same way about me. I went to grief counselling. Now I see it as a not a bug but a feature. The receipts for love are grief and anxiety."

This is the key to the entire podcast — and perhaps the entire AI moment. Galloway, who has been clinically dissecting a world where human labour is obsolete, where billionaires plan their escapes, and where tech messiahs are revealed as capitalists, is himself a demonstration of the one thing that cannot be automated: the capacity to grieve, to love, and to find meaning in relationships that will never produce a positive ROI.

He says it himself, almost as an aside: "I will never get a positive return for my children. They're never going to be up at 2 a.m. worried about me. They are costing me so much money. The amount of love, concern, and anxiety I feel over my boys — it would be almost impossible for them to return that. And that's the point."

This is the argument that the dystopian novels always make and the tech optimists always miss. Meaning does not come from productivity. It does not come from efficiency. It does not come from optimizing returns. It comes from the irrational, uneconomical, deeply human act of caring about something so much that you would be devastated by its loss.

## VIII. The Reckoning That Isn't Coming

Galloway is asked whether an economic crash is coming. He thinks it might be — perhaps triggered by what he calls "AI dumping," where Chinese companies flood the market with cheap AI models and the absurd valuations of American AI companies collapse. He notes that 40% of the S&P 500 is now "directly or tangentially related to this giant bet America is making on AI."

But the more important question isn't whether the market crashes. It's whether a crash would change anything. The 2008 financial crisis destroyed trillions in wealth, displaced millions of workers, and revealed systemic corruption. The result? The banks were bailed out. The executives kept their bonuses. The displaced workers were left to rebuild on their own.

Galloway himself notes this: "We bailed out every baby boomer owner of a restaurant or a small business in COVID. That just robbed opportunity from the new graduate of a culinary academy who wanted her shot to go buy a restaurant for pennies on the dollar."

The system — by which I mean the political-economic arrangement that protects asset values for the old and eliminates opportunity for the young — is remarkably resilient. It survives crashes. It survives pandemics. It may well survive the AI displacement. Not because it is just, but because the people who benefit from it have the resources to ensure its survival — including, as Galloway notes, a disproportionate share of political donations.

## IX. The Real Question

Galloway's podcast is being discussed as a warning about AI. It is actually something more unsettling: a warning about *us*. About what happens when a species that has organized its entire civilization around productive labour confronts the possibility that productive labour is no longer necessary — not because everyone is free, but because a small number of people own the machines that do the work and have no intention of sharing the proceeds.

The dystopia is not that AI will become conscious and destroy us. The dystopia is that AI will remain exactly what it is — a tool owned by the few, deployed to eliminate the economic necessity of the many, while the owners retreat to bunkers and the displaced are left to make meaning in a world that has told them they have none.

Galloway, to his credit, does not end there. He ends with his mother. He ends with his sons. He ends with the argument that purpose is found in the things you cannot get a return on — love, grief, anxiety, connection. It is a beautiful argument. It may even be true.

But it is not an economic argument. It will not pay the rent. It will not employ the analyst replaced by Molly. It will not regulate Sam Altman. And it will not stop the billionaire from meeting his pilot at Oakland airport.

The question Galloway's podcast ultimately poses is not whether AI will destroy us. It is whether we are capable of building a society where human worth is decoupled from economic output *before* the people who control the machines decide we are no longer worth the investment.

History is not encouraging.
