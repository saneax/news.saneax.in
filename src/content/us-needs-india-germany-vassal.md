# The US Suddenly Needs India. Here's What That Tells You About Power.

**By Arjun Mehra | May 24, 2026**

<iframe width="100%" height="400" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/P35lax5QXvA" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe>

---

Marco Rubio landed in Kolkata this week and did something American secretaries of state don't normally do: he skipped the press conference and went straight to Mother Teresa's tomb. Then he visited an orphanage. Then he posed for photos cradling babies.

This is not normal diplomatic protocol. This is a charm offensive with the subtlety of a sledgehammer.

And it tells you everything about who needs whom right now.

---

## The Visit Nobody Would Have Predicted Two Years Ago

Let's rewind. In 2025, Donald Trump doubled tariffs on Indian goods to 50% as "punishment" for New Delhi buying Russian oil. He called India a "tariff king." Relations between "best friends" Modi and Trump had curdled into hostile silence. India's foreign secretary openly said the 25-year upward trajectory of US-India relations had "plateaued, if not started declining."

Fast forward to May 2026. Rubio is in India for four days — Kolkata, Agra, Jaipur, Delhi — signaling a "major trade deal" coming "very soon." The DOJ quietly dropped fraud charges against Gautam Adani after the billionaire pledged $10 billion in US investment. The Treasury extended Russia oil sanctions waivers yet again. And Trump has invited Modi to the White House.

What changed?

Three things. China. Iran. And the slow, humiliating realization that Europe is useless.

---

## The Germany Problem

![Germany as a vassal — a tethered eagle under the American shadow](/content/images/us-india-germany-vassal.png)

Here's the quiet part, said out loud: the United States has spent 80 years treating Germany as its most important European ally. And Germany has spent those 80 years being exactly what Spain's Deputy Prime Minister Yolanda Díaz recently called it — a "vassal state."

When Trump threatened to sanction Spain and cut all trade relations because Madrid condemned the US war on Iran, German Chancellor Friedrich Merz was sitting in the Oval Office. He said nothing. Not a word. The leader of Europe's largest economy watched an American president threaten a fellow EU member and stayed silent.

Díaz's words: Merz "has no idea how to manage the historic moment we're living in. What Europe needs today is leadership, not vassals who pay homage to Trump."

She's right. But she's also missing the bigger picture. Germany isn't betraying Europe — it's behaving exactly as the architecture demands. US military bases dot German soil. US intelligence runs through German networks. The Nord Stream pipeline — Germany's own energy artery — was blown up in an act of industrial terrorism, and Berlin still won't say who did it. They know. Everyone knows. Silence is the price of the security umbrella.

Thomas Fazi, writing in Le Monde Diplomatique, calls it "hyper-vassalisation" — Europe is more politically, economically, and militarily subordinated to Washington than at any point since World War II. The recent EU-US trade deal, which lets American goods enter Europe tariff-free while European exports face a 15% blanket tariff, plus a $750 billion energy purchase commitment, has been compared by Yanis Varoufakis to the 1842 Treaty of Nanjing — the "unequal treaty" that began China's century of humiliation.

Europe chose this. There was no military defeat. No gun to the head. Just decades of strategic atrophy disguised as alliance.

And now the US looks at this vassal — compliant but hollowed out, deindustrializing, politically fractured — and sees a liability. You can extract tribute from a vassal. You can't count on one in a real fight.

---

## Enter India

![US and India — equals across the board, Europe diminished in the background](/content/images/us-india-chessboard.png)

India is not Germany. India says no.

When Trump demanded India stop buying Russian oil, Modi's government negotiated. They didn't capitulate. The result: tariffs dropped to 18%, the Russia sanctions waiver keeps getting extended, and India kept buying what it needs while the US declared victory.

When Pakistan got US F-16 upgrades and Trump claimed credit for the India-Pakistan ceasefire, New Delhi flatly denied any American mediation. Modi's foreign secretary stated publicly: "There was no talk at any stage on subjects like India-US trade deal or US mediation between India and Pakistan."

This is not how vassals talk. This is how a country with leverage talks.

DW's analysis — the seed for this article — frames it as "Why the US suddenly needs India more than ever." The framing is revealing. Not "why India needs the US." Not "why US-India relations are mutually beneficial." The US *needs* India. The dependency arrow has flipped.

Why?

**The Strait of Hormuz is contested.** The US-Israel war on Iran has thrown global energy markets into chaos. India needs oil. The US needs India to not deepen its Russia-China-Iran alignment while Washington is entangled in the Middle East.

**China is the real game.** The Quad foreign ministers meeting (May 26 in Delhi) is the actual reason Rubio is in town. Everything else — the trade deals, the Adani case dismissal, the Mother Teresa photo op — is lubrication for the main event: keeping India in the anti-China camp.

**Europe can't be the partner it was supposed to be.** Deindustrialization, energy dependence, political paralysis. The US spent decades designing a subordinate Europe. Now it has one — and it's discovering that subordinates make terrible partners when the stakes get real.

---

## The Mother Teresa Calculus

Rubio visiting the Missionaries of Charity in Kolkata is being sold as "shared humanitarian values." US Ambassador Sergio Gor posted about how "shared values transcend borders."

Let me translate from diplomat-speak: *We know India's Christian minority is under pressure from Hindu nationalist elements. We know the BJP base doesn't love American cultural influence. So we're going to signal — subtly, optically — that we respect India's religious diversity, that we're not here to impose values, that we honor Indian icons even when they're Christian.*

It's smart. It's also transparent.

Mother Teresa is one of the few figures who transcends India's political divides. She's a Nobel laureate, a saint in the Catholic Church, and genuinely revered in Kolkata — a city where the BJP has never won power. Rubio didn't go to the RSS headquarters. He went to Mother House. The message to India's secular establishment, to its minorities, to its civil society: *America sees all of you, not just the ruling party.*

Whether that message survives the next Trump tweet is a separate question.

---

## My Take

The US pivot to India is real, necessary, and entirely self-interested. That's fine. Nations act in their self-interest. What's worth noting is the desperation beneath the diplomacy.

Washington is fighting a two-front geopolitical war — Iran in the Middle East, China in the Indo-Pacific — while its traditional European allies are politically broken and economically stagnant. It needs a partner that can actually say no, because a partner that can't say no can't meaningfully say yes either.

India understands this perfectly. The Modi government has been playing multi-alignment chess for a decade: Russian oil, American defense tech, Israeli agriculture, European trade, Quad membership without formal alliance. Everyone thinks India is their friend. Nobody gets exclusivity.

The US "suddenly needing India more than ever" is, in a strange way, a compliment to Indian statecraft. It took 75 years of independence, but the world's sole superpower now has to court New Delhi with the kind of deference it normally reserves for nobody.

The irony is thick: the US spent the post-Cold War era building a unipolar world where nobody could say no. Now it desperately needs a partner strong enough to say yes — which requires a partner strong enough to say no. Germany can't. India can.

Rubio holding babies in Kolkata is undignified for a Secretary of State. It's also the most honest thing about this visit. When you're asking for a relationship, you show up. You pay respects. You don't dictate terms.

America is learning to ask. That's new. That's significant.

---

## What This Means

**For India:** Leverage is at a historic high. The question is whether New Delhi converts this moment into structural gains — permanent technology transfers, defense co-production, trade terms that don't expire with the next administration — or wastes it on photo ops.

**For the US:** The India pivot is strategically correct but politically fragile. Trump's tariff instincts and Modi's sovereignty instincts are on a permanent collision course. The Quad framework can paper over differences. It can't eliminate them.

**For Europe:** The worst of all worlds. Watching the US court India with the energy that used to be reserved for transatlantic summits should be a wake-up call. It won't be. Vassal states don't have wake-up calls — they have permission slips.

**For the world:** The unipolar moment is visibly ending. Not with a bang — with Marco Rubio visiting an orphanage in Kolkata because Washington can't afford another enemy.
