# The Quiet Winners of Every War You Watch on Television

There's a video on YouTube with 3.1 million views. It's called "Who's Really Pulling the Strings in Global Wars." Johnny Harris made it — the same Johnny Harris whose Middle East documentary hit 5.4 million views this March, making it the most-watched geopolitical long-form video of 2026 so far.

The video is about proxy wars. Who starts them. Who fights them. And — here's the uncomfortable part — who profits from them.

The timing couldn't be better. Or worse, depending on where you sit.

## What Harris Actually Shows

Harris walks through how modern warfare has become a business model. Not a conspiracy theory — a documented, audited, publicly-traded business model. He traces how the United States and other powers use proxies to fight wars without putting their own soldiers in body bags. Ukraine. Yemen. Syria. Now Iran.

The argument isn't new. What's new is that 3.1 million people watched a 29-minute explainer about defense contractor profits — and 71,000 of them liked it. That's not a niche audience anymore. That's mainstream.

## The Receipts

Let's look at the numbers Harris's video points toward, and what's happened since he published it in March 2025:

**Lockheed Martin** reported 2025 sales of **$75 billion** — up 6% from the previous year. Net earnings: $5 billion. The company's 2026 outlook? More growth. Reuters reported in January that Lockheed set a "profit-sharing deal" with the US government that pleased investors.

**RTX (Raytheon)** delivered what it called "strong 2025 sales, EPS, and free cash flow" and projected continued growth in 2026.

Al Jazeera investigated which US and Israeli military companies are profiting from the Iran war and found that the biggest defense companies "agreed to quadruple production" of what Trump described as critical munitions.

So here's the math: the US is fighting a war in Iran. American defense contractors are quadrupling production. Their stock prices are up. Their earnings calls are optimistic. And the American taxpayer is funding all of it.

This isn't opinion. This is from their own financial filings.

## But Not Everyone's Convinced

Harris has his critics — and they're serious. The Kyiv Independent published a scathing piece titled "YouTuber Johnny Harris's lens on Eastern Europe is distorted and irresponsible." It accused him of "echoing Kremlin talking points about NATO expansionism" and "trading facts for sensationalism."

The criticism matters because it points to the central tension in Harris's work: when you make geopolitics cinematic, something gets lost in the editing. Maps are simplified. Nuance is compressed. And sometimes, in the pursuit of a clean narrative, you end up making the aggressor's arguments sound reasonable.

This is the line Harris walks — and the line that 7.9 million subscribers watch him walk every week.

## The Side Stories Nobody's Covering

Dig into the comments, the Reddit threads, the X conversations, and patterns emerge:

**The recruiter problem.** Multiple users point out that defense contractors don't just profit from wars — they help *start* them by placing former generals and admirals on their boards, who then advocate for military intervention through think tanks and media appearances. One commenter on X noted: "Lockheed has more former Pentagon officials on payroll than the Pentagon has active duty generals." We couldn't verify that exact claim. But the revolving-door data is public.

**The India angle nobody talks about.** India isn't a major arms exporter. But as global supply chains reconfigure around the Iran conflict and the broader Middle East instability, Indian defense stocks have surged. HAL, Bharat Dynamics, Mazagon Dock — all at or near all-time highs in 2026. War is bad for people. It's very good for defense balance sheets. That math doesn't change with geography.

**The audience is getting smarter.** One trend visible across YouTube comments on geopolitical content: viewers are increasingly asking "who benefits?" before "who's right?" The comments section of Harris's video isn't filled with flag-waving. It's filled with people asking about defense contractor stock prices, about the military-industrial complex, about why America fights wars it can't seem to finish. Whether you agree with Harris or not, his audience is doing something most cable news viewers don't: they're following the money.

## What We Know

- **Defense contractors are having record years.** Lockheed Martin: $75B in 2025 sales. RTX: strong growth. Production quadrupling.
- **The Iran war is accelerating this.** Al Jazeera documented which companies are directly benefiting.
- **Johnny Harris's audience is enormous and growing.** 7.9M subscribers, a 5.4M-view Middle East documentary, and a 3.1M-view explainer on proxy wars.
- **The "follow the money" framing resonates.** Viewers are engaging with structural analysis of war, not just flag-waving.

## What We Don't Know

- **How much of this is Harris's genuine analysis versus narrative packaging?** The Kyiv Independent's criticism isn't trivial. When you simplify 70 years of NATO-Russia history into 30 minutes, what gets cut?
- **What happens when the wars end?** Defense contractors are structured for permanent war. What does "peacetime" look like for a company that just quadrupled production?
- **Is anyone in Washington asking these questions?** Tucker Carlson is. A handful of anti-war Republicans are. But the defense budget keeps going up, and the wars keep spreading.

## The Question Harris Doesn't Answer

If proxy wars are a business model, who's the customer?

The answer, if you follow the money, isn't the American people. It isn't the soldiers fighting. It's the shareholders — of which the US government, through its pension funds and procurement contracts, is both the biggest investor and the biggest client.

That's not a conspiracy. That's just a business model that happens to run on human lives.
